# Fire Crackers — Content Strategy

---

## Overview

Content is not a support function for the Fire Crackers brand — it is the brand. The product is what you eat. The universe is why you care. The goal is to build an audience that would follow these characters even without a product to sell, and then convert that audience into customers, fans, and eventually cult followers.

Two parallel tracks run simultaneously:
- **Short-form social content** — fast, reactive, platform-native, character-led
- **The animated mini-series** — serialized, universe-building, longer-form

Everything feeds each other. An episode of the mini-series generates 6–10 social clips. A social clip teases the next episode. A character introduced on TikTok gets their own can at launch. A limited edition flavor release becomes an episode.

---

## The Universe: "Against All Blandness"

### The World

The Fire Crackers operate out of a converted tobacco factory in an unnamed Bulgarian town — gritty, warm, falling apart in all the right ways. It is part workshop, part war room, part home. The crew makes the world's most unapologetic snacks here, by hand, with ingredients sourced from Bulgarian farms, mountains, and markets.

They operate in deliberate defiance of **the Corporate Flavor Authority (CFA)** — a faceless regulatory body dedicated to making all food safe, mild, shelf-stable, and completely forgettable. The CFA is not evil in a cartoonish way. It is worse than that: it is bureaucratic. It genuinely believes bland is better. It sends inspectors. It files paperwork. It is the natural enemy of everything Fire Crackers stands for.

The CFA's field operative is **Mr. Bland** — a plain unsalted cracker in a corporate suit who has never experienced flavor and considers this a professional asset. He is not incompetent. He is the perfect antagonist: methodical, relentless, and completely unable to understand why anyone would want anything to hurt this much going down.

### The Tone

The show is not a children's cartoon. It is closer to: early seasons of Archer, with the visual language of Cuphead, set in Bulgaria, about snacks. Fast dialogue, physical comedy, occasional genuine emotion played completely straight. The characters are ridiculous. They take themselves completely seriously. The audience is always in on the joke.

## The Cast in Story Context

| Character | Role in the Universe |
|---|---|
| Blaze | De facto leader — nobody voted for him, he just assumed the position. Usually right. Occasionally catastrophically wrong. |
| Matchstick | Does the prep work. Knows where everything is. Runs fast. The only one who takes notes. |
| Ha-Hank | Handles anything that requires brute force or a very confident bluff. Slow to anger, then fast. |
| Carolina | The strategist when Blaze won't admit he needs one. Usually the reason any plan actually works. Lets Blaze believe it was his idea. |
| Ghost | Intelligence and reconnaissance. Nobody knows exactly when he joined. Nobody asks. |
| Abi | Research, precision, anything requiring a cold head. Has a low tolerance for chaos but remains functional within it. |
| Shipka | Has seen all of this before. Provides the only adult supervision available, which is not much. Factory was his originally. |
| Smoky | Procurement — ingredients, contacts, logistics. Knows every supplier, farmer, and bar owner within 200km. Never explains how. |

**Mr. Bland** appears in 1 of every 3–4 pieces of content. Enough to maintain menace. Not so much that he becomes familiar.

## Platforms & Roles

### TikTok — Primary Reach Engine
The place Fire Crackers finds new audiences. Organic reach first — pay to amplify only what already performs. Fast, reactive, leans into platform trends while staying visually distinct.

Content here is: short (15–60 seconds), character-driven, often a single joke or moment, designed to be rewatched.

**Posting cadence:** 5x per week

---

### Instagram — Primary Brand Home
The visual portfolio. Feed is the brand identity at a glance — someone landing on the profile for the first time should understand the entire world in 9 squares. Stories are the daily conversation. Reels mirror best-performing TikTok content.

**Posting cadence:** Feed 3x per week, Stories daily

---

### YouTube — The Mini-Series Home
Episodes live here. Also extended cuts and behind-the-scenes of the animation process. Slower growth but builds the deepest fans — the ones who will buy every LTO and own three pieces of merch.

**Posting cadence:** 1 episode per month (2–5 minutes). Supplementary clips 2x per week.

---

### Facebook — Bulgarian Market Reach
Facebook remains disproportionately strong in Bulgaria. Mirror Instagram content. Run targeted paid campaigns here for bar owner outreach and local consumer acquisition. Not a creative-first platform for this brand — it is distribution.

**Posting cadence:** Mirror Instagram, 3x per week

## Content Pillars

### 1. Character Universe
The primary pillar. Short animated clips, character moments, interactions, reactions. The crew getting into situations. Each piece of character content builds the world without requiring the audience to have seen anything before.

Formats: 15–30 second animated clips, character reaction loops, "a day in the life of [character]" shorts, character vs. character moments.

Goal: audience develops loyalty to specific characters. They pick a favorite. They defend that choice online.

---

### 2. Mixed Media — "Real World Visits"
The crew enters the real world. Animated characters composited over real footage, or real people's faces replaced with animated character faces. Reaction content, bar visits, ingredient sourcing trips — all with animation on top.

Formats: Product reaction videos (real person + animated face overlay + animated effects), "Blaze visits a bar" (animated Blaze composited into real bar footage), animated characters reacting to real events.

Goal: bridges the animated universe and the real product. Makes the brand feel alive in both worlds.

Production note: face replacement requires compositing work — plan for a 2–3 day turnaround per mixed media piece. Build a small backlog before launch.

---

### 3. The Mission Dispatches
Every limited edition / seasonal flavor launch is a mission. The content around an LTO release is structured as mission briefings, in-progress updates, and debrief recaps — all in character and universe.

Formats: Animated mission briefing (30–60 sec, voiced in character), behind-the-scenes "field report" of sourcing the special ingredient, mission debrief after the batch sells out.

Goal: makes every product drop a narrative event, not just a product announcement.

---

### 4. Behind the Fire
The real side of the brand. Ingredient sourcing from Bulgarian farms and markets. The factory. The recipe testing. Shipka is the natural host character for this pillar — his groundedness suits it.

Formats: Short documentary-style clips with animation overlay, farmer/supplier features (faces replaced with character face in the Shipka aesthetic), "where the heat comes from" origin stories per ingredient.

Goal: builds authenticity and the "made in Bulgaria" story without abandoning the brand voice.

---

### 5. Bar Culture
Fire Crackers belongs in bars. Content anchors the brand in bar settings — real Sofia bars, craft beer culture, late nights.

Formats: "Find us at [bar name]" placement content, animated characters appearing in real bar environments, bartender collaborations (their face = an animated character face), pairing suggestions delivered in character voice.

Goal: drives bar distribution by making bars want to be associated with the brand, not just stock it.

---

### 6. Community & Provocation
Audience participation. Polls, challenges, "which character are you," flavor name voting for LTOs, heat tolerance rankings. Designed to generate comments, shares, and UGC.

Formats: Instagram polls, TikTok challenge formats, "submit your Fire Crackers face" UGC campaigns (where the brand animates submitted user photos into character-style art for the best entries).

Goal: converts passive viewers into active community members.

## The Animated Mini-Series

### Format
- Episode length: 2–5 minutes
- Release: Monthly
- Platform: YouTube primary, cut-down clips on TikTok/Instagram
- Style: 1930s rubber-hose animation, voiced characters, original music

### Season 1 Arc: "Open for Business"

The first season establishes the world, introduces the crew, and ends with the Fire Crackers making it into their first real bar — against every obstacle the CFA places in their way.

**8 episodes. One per character introduced, plus a two-part finale.**

---

**Episode 1 — "The Factory"**
Establishing the world. Blaze walks us through the factory, introducing it with unearned confidence. We meet Shipka (the factory is his; he's been making crackers here for decades). Blaze has a plan to make the hottest snack in Bulgaria. Shipka has heard this before. The CFA receives an anonymous tip about "unauthorized flavor escalation" somewhere in the country.

---

**Episode 2 — "Matchstick Runs"**
Matchstick is sent on a supply run to a local market. Simple task. Goes immediately sideways. We learn everything about Matchstick through watching him fail at something small with enormous commitment. He succeeds eventually, in a way nobody anticipated. Mr. Bland makes his first appearance — observing from a distance, filing a form.

---

**Episode 3 — "Ha-Hank's Problem"**
Ha-Hank has been negotiating with a habanero supplier for three weeks. The deal is almost done. There is a misunderstanding. The misunderstanding escalates. Ha-Hank's slow burn finally peaks. Everything works out but two things are on fire that weren't on fire before. The supplier becomes a recurring character.

---

**Episode 4 — "Ghost of the Factory"**
The crew realizes they don't know when Ghost joined. They don't know where he sleeps. They're not entirely sure he's always been there. A CFA inspection is coming. Ghost handles it. Nobody sees him do it. The inspectors leave looking mildly confused. Ghost is making tea in the kitchen when they get back, like nothing happened.

---

**Episode 5 — "Abi's Formula"**
Abi has been working on a recipe for six weeks. It's perfect. She knows it's perfect. The problem is getting the rest of the crew to test it without them immediately ruining the controlled conditions. A precision comedy of the methodical versus the chaotic. Carolina is the only one Abi trusts to take notes correctly.

---

**Episode 6 — "Carolina's Reputation"**
A bar owner in Sofia has heard of Carolina Reaper crackers. He wants them. He also thinks he can handle them. The crew debates whether to warn him. Carolina says nothing. Blaze says yes. We follow the delivery. The bar owner's reaction becomes the first real piece of mixed media content: his face replaced with an animated face, the cartoon reaction layered on top.

---

**Episode 7 — "Smoky's Contacts"**
Smoky needs to call in a favor to get the crew access to a smoked paprika source that doesn't officially exist on any supplier list. This episode is a series of encounters with increasingly strange characters across a single day. We never fully understand Smoky's history with any of them. He gets what he came for. He buys everyone drinks at the end.

---

**Episode 8 — "The First Bar" (Part 1)**
The CFA has identified the factory. Mr. Bland files for a Flavor Escalation Citation. The crew has 48 hours to get product into at least one bar before the paperwork goes through — because once they're a functioning business in a licensed venue, the CFA's jurisdiction changes. A race. Every character gets a role. Everything that can go wrong does. Ends on a cliffhanger.

---

**Episode 9 — "The First Bar" (Part 2)**
The resolution. Product makes it into the bar. The CFA citation is filed — one day too late. Mr. Bland stands outside the bar looking at the Fire Crackers display behind the glass. He takes out a new form. He starts filling it in. The crew celebrates inside. Blaze takes credit. Carolina smiles slightly. Shipka buys the first round. Matchstick sets a small thing on fire by accident. Season ends.

---

### Season 2 Seed
The season 1 finale plants: Mr. Bland is not filing a citation. He's filing a different form. Something bigger. Season 2 begins with a knock on the factory door.

## Pre-Launch Content Plan (Smoke Test Phase)

Before any product is widely available. The goal: build audience, test aesthetic resonance, generate waitlist signups.

### Week 1–2: The Signal
No characters. No product. Just atmosphere.

- Post 1: A close-up of the wax seal being stamped. No context. No caption except the logo.
- Post 2: A close-up of a burning wick. Still no context.
- Post 3: A short 5-second animation loop — the Fire Crackers wordmark assembles itself in the retro style, bursts into flame, grins.
- Bio: "Something's burning. Coming soon." Link: waitlist page.

Goal: intrigue. Early followers are self-selected — they engaged with zero context. They are the core audience.

---

### Week 3–4: Meet the Crew
One character reveal per day, alternating platforms. Each reveal is a short 10–15 second animated clip — the character appears, does one characteristic thing, disappears. No narration. No product mention.

Order: Blaze (Monday), Matchstick (Wednesday), Ha-Hank (Friday), Carolina (Monday), Ghost (Wednesday — he appears in the background of the Carolina post a day before his official reveal, visible to anyone who looks), Abi (Friday), Shipka (Sunday), Smoky (Tuesday).

Caption format: just the character name. Nothing else. Let the audience ask questions in comments — do not answer them directly. Let other audience members speculate.

---

### Week 5–6: The World
First environment content. The factory. Short clips of the space — warm, textured, worn, slightly on fire in places. Blaze walking through it. A bag of crackers visible for the first time, on a shelf, never the focus of the shot.

Introduce the waitlist push: "First batch going somewhere. Join the list to find out where." Link in bio.

---

### Week 7–8: Proof of Life
The prototype 50–100 bags land in 2–3 Sofia bars. Content: Blaze (animated) composited into the real bar environment. "Find us." Location tagged. Real bar, real product, real people reacting — animated face overlays on the reactions.

Waitlist email: "We're live. Limited. Here's where."

---

## Launch Phase Content

Once product is in bars and the online store is live:

**Week 1:** Blaze announces the launch — in character, animated, the least corporate launch announcement possible.

**Week 2:** Bar content. Which bars, why those bars, what the bar owners think (animated face overlay interviews).

**Week 3:** First heat reaction content — real people, animated overlays, Blaze appearing in frame to take credit.

**Week 4:** Episode 1 of the mini-series drops on YouTube. Every platform gets a different clip from it.

**Month 2:** First LTO teaser begins. 10-second animated clip. One character. One ingredient hint. Nothing else. The mission briefing format activates.

## Content Production Setup

### Minimum production infrastructure at launch:
- 1 animator / motion designer: retro style specialist. Commissions monthly episode + 8–12 social clips per month from provided character assets
- 1 short-form video editor: handles mixed media compositing and social content assembly
- 1 copywriter / content manager: handles captions, community responses, content calendar
- Photography: product shots, bar placement documentation — single shoot per month minimum

### Asset pipeline:
Character assets (rigged or layered PSD/AI files) are created once as part of the brand identity commission and reused across all animation. This is the highest-leverage investment in the entire content operation — well-built character rigs reduce per-clip animation time from days to hours.

### Content volume targets (monthly, ongoing):
- 20+ TikTok posts
- 12–14 Instagram feed posts
- 30 Stories
- 4–6 Reels / TikTok cross-posts
- 1 YouTube episode
- 4–6 YouTube supplementary clips
- 12–14 Facebook posts (mirrored)

## Influencer & Seeding Strategy

### Who to target
Not traditional food influencers. The right audience for Fire Crackers is:

- **Bar and nightlife accounts** in Sofia — the distribution channel and the cultural home
- **Craft beer and drinks content creators** — the adjacent culture
- **Streetwear / urban lifestyle** accounts — the merch audience
- **Animation and design enthusiasts** — will share the art style
- **Expat and diaspora Bulgarians** — will share the local pride story globally
- **Food challenge creators** — organic fit for heat content, use carefully (Paqui lesson applies — no medically extreme framing)
- **Tattoo artists (American Traditional / Neo-Traditional)** — the brand's visual DNA is the same as theirs. Approach as peers, not as sponsors. Send product + the flash sheet print. Do not send a brief. If they post, it's because they genuinely connect with the art — which is the only kind of tattoo community endorsement that lands. A tattooed character from a genuine artist in this style is more brand equity than any paid placement. Priority targets: Sofia-based artists first, then regional.

### Approach
- Send product + a character-specific handwritten note (in character voice from that character)
- No briefing document. No required hashtags. No mandatory post format.
- If they post organically, engage authentically.
- If they want a paid arrangement, discuss. Do not require it upfront.

### Micro over macro
Target 20–30 creators with 2,000–30,000 followers in the right niches over 2–3 with 200,000+. The niche audience converts. The mass audience follows and forgets.

## Metrics That Matter

| Metric | Why |
|---|---|
| Saves (Instagram) | Strongest signal of genuine interest — people saving to return to |
| Shares / reposts | Unprompted distribution — the content earned it |
| Comments asking where to buy | Direct purchase intent signal |
| Waitlist signups | Measured purchase intent |
| Profile visits from content | The content drives brand curiosity |
| Bar reorder rate | The ultimate real-world content validation |
| Character fan expressions (fan art, references) | The universe has taken root |

**Vanity metrics to deprioritize:** raw follower count, total impressions, likes without saves.

---

## What to Avoid

- **Trend-chasing that breaks character.** If a TikTok trend requires abandoning the retro aesthetic or the character voice, skip it. The distinctiveness is the strategy.
- **Overexplaining the brand.** Never caption "Fire Crackers is a hot snack brand that..." — the content explains the brand. If it doesn't, fix the content.
- **Responding to every comment in brand voice if the response is forced.** Silence is preferable to a clunky brand response. Engage when it's genuinely funny or useful.
- **Treating the mini-series as secondary.** The series is the long game. Short-form is acquisition. Long-form is retention. Both matter equally.
- **Making Mr. Bland sympathetic too early.** He can be humanized in Season 2. In Season 1 he is the obstacle. Play it straight.
